"I hear that I am limestone by the sea” explores the boundaries of senses, materiality, and time through the study of limestone situated between ocean and land.

Shaped over millennia by geological transformations, high temperatures, immense pressure, and the accumulation of biological remnants, limestone can be seen as a dynamic archive—one that stores and carries environmental data, including tectonic shifts, ecological changes, and geological and biological processes across oceanic and planetary scales, along with information yet to be deciphered. These archives are not closed or static but porous, permeable, and open, constantly exchanging with their surroundings. Through interactions with living organisms, water currents, waves, erosion, and renewal, limestone perpetually reinterprets its environment, forming a fluid epistemic field. We might imagine that limestone is always in the process of re-learning water, re-learning the ocean, re-learning the land.

The work engages with the materiality, structure, and composition of limestone, weaving ecological and geological thinking with personal affections and embodied contemplation.

The main narrative of the work shows the dreamlike stream of consciousness of a carbonate stone, presented through video, 3D animation, and text on a floating monitor. Alongside this, a dual-screen installation features 3D-scanned limestone textures, processed through surface unwrapping techniques and digital manipulation.

The sounds were recorded using a hydrophone placed inside limestone cavities, imagines listening from the stone’s perspective, imagines the act of listening from the stone’s perspective, its openness resonating with the shifting boundary between land and sea, as well as between the interior and exterior of bodies.
